3.1 Run the two-way factorial ANOVA

We will now run our factorial ANOVA using the same code as in Lab 4. As a reminder, from our results, we see that there are significant main effects on social participation for special educational needs (F(1, 1329) = 54.04, p < .001) and country (F(2, 1329) = 7.00, p < .001), and the interaction effect between special educational needs and country was not significant (F(2, 1329) = 1.43, p = .239). 4. Factorial ANOVA Interaction Contrasts

In section 3, we answered the question of whether the effect of sen2 on soc_part significantly differed between levels of country, finding no evidence to support our hypothesis. However, we can further probe our interactions by running post-hoc contrasts. Similar to what we practiced in Lab 3, we will apply post-hoc contrasts to extend our understanding of between group variance. Applying interaction contrasts will be the core of completing your analyses for Lab 5.

We will start by defining a post-hoc hypothesis based on the mean differences observed in the descriptive statistics and data visualizations:
* Hypothesis: The mean level of ‘social participation’ for ‘typically developing’ and ‘special needs’ significantly differ between Norway vs. Belgium and the Netherlands.

4.1 Defining contrasts

Because this is a 2x3 two-way factorial ANOVA, we need to provide 6 contrast coefficients for each hypothesized contrast comparison, one for each combination between our two IVs. The code below has notes to clarify the contrast coefficient values.

contrasts <- list(
  hyp1 = c(-1, -1, 2, 1, 1, -2)) # Hypothesis 1: contrasting social interaction scores of typically developing and spec. needs between Norway vs. Belgium and the Netherlands.

m <- contrast(means_2, contrasts, adjust = "holm")
test(m, joint = TRUE)
##  df1  df2 F.ratio p.value
##    1 1331  25.053  <.0001

Before we interpret the results of the interaction contrast, we need to calculate the new \(\alpha\) threshold to correct for multiple comparisons inflating our Type 1 Familywise Error Rate. Counting the initial two-way factorial ANOVA and the post-hoc contrast, the new significance threshold for p is .025 (i.e., \(\alpha\) = .05/2 tests = .025).

4.3 Interpreting Interaction Contrast Results

Back to our interaction contrast results: Our findings indicate that the effect of special needs categorization (typically developing vs. special needs) on social participation significantly differed between Norway versus Belgium and the Netherlands (t = 5.005, p < .001, \(\omega^{2}\) = 0.018). In other words, Norway has a wider disparity in social inclusion between students with special needs and their typically developing peers than Belgium and the Netherlands. However, the differences between these countries and the levels of special needs only account for an estimated 1.8% of variance in social participation.
